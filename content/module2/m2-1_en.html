Exploring culture

Culture can be defined as the shared values, norms, family styles, social roles and behaviours of a group of people. The people may have developed these common traits in response to the political, economic, environmental and social realities they face. Culture also refers to the things they share based on common ethnicity, race and/or nationhood.

To further explore culture as a concept, let’s look more closely at what is easily seen and what is less visible beneath the surface. We’ll use the iceberg as a metaphor to present these layers of culture. As you know, an iceberg can be very large, with only 10% of the ice showing above the water. The remainder is hidden below. Culture can be viewed in much the same way.

Think about people who come from a culture that is different from your own. Certain things are easily seen as being different. These characteristics are represented by the tip of the iceberg (Category A).

Some differences about a culture may take more time to recognize. These are represented by the ice just below the water’s surface (Category B).

Certain aspects of a culture are difficult to see. You would only come to recognize them if you closely associated with people from that culture. These are represented in the deepest layer of the iceberg (Category C).

Self-reflection: Take a moment to think about your culture and the community and family that raised you. Using the iceberg model as a reference, think about how you might describe your culture. Consider each of the following aspects and think of specific examples:

  • food and music, including at community celebrations
  • family traditions
  • style of communication and language
  • family and family roles
  • values and beliefs
  • perceptions of justice
  • how you express humour
  • other attributes of your culture
