Before we begin, a note on terminology

The terms we use in our work and day-to-day interactions influence what we do and the views we carry. As cultures and social norms change, so does our language. The words used to describe Indigenous peoples are continuously evolving.

Understanding language and word use can be a challenge for many people. The following definitions aim to help you understand some of the key terms and how to use them properly.

Aboriginal peoples of Canada

This term is defined in section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982. It states that Aboriginal peoples “includes the Indian, Inuit and Métis peoples of Canada.” These are 3 separate groups of peoples who have unique heritages, languages, cultural practices and spiritual beliefs. The term “Aboriginal” has specific meaning and legal accuracy in the context of section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982.

First Peoples

This is a collective term that describes the original peoples who inhabited Canada and their descendants. It is used less frequently than terms like “Aboriginal peoples” and “Indigenous peoples.”

Indigenous peoples

These are communities, peoples and nations who consider themselves distinct for having a historically continuous presence in Canada before the land was colonized. They have their own cultures, social institutions and legal systems.

The terms “Indigenous peoples” and “Aboriginal peoples” are the same. However, they are not interchangeable within the legal context. The Government of Canada generally uses the term “Indigenous peoples” to refer to the First Nations, Inuit and Métis of Canada. Unlike the term “Aboriginal peoples,” the term “Indigenous peoples” has not been legally defined in statute or by the courts in Canada.

Terms for First Nations, Métis and Inuit will be covered in Courses 3, 4 and 5.
